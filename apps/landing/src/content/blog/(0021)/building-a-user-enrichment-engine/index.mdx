Here is a signup that broke our first enrichment system:

Jane signs up with `jane@gmail.com`. Her LinkedIn says she's **CTO at Acme** — and also
**advisor at Beta Corp**, and she still lists a **board seat at Gamma**. Beta Corp is a
customer of ours. Acme is a prospect we've been trying to crack for a year.

A conventional enrichment schema — one row per user, one `job_title` column — has to pick.
Whatever the provider guesses as her "primary" title wins, the other two employers evaporate,
and with them the single most interesting fact in this signup: *someone connected to a
current customer just signed up, and she runs engineering at a target account.*

That's the actual problem with user enrichment, and it's a schema problem, not a data
problem. **A lead is not a person. A lead is a person × an employer.** The moment you accept
that, the architecture follows: three tables, each doing one job.

## Table 1: `enriched_users` — one row per signup, forever

Your application pushes three columns the moment someone registers: `user_id` (your id — it
becomes the join key for everything), `name`, `email`. Nothing is ever deleted from this
table; enriched or not, it's your census.

Everything else on it is machinery. The full standardized profile as one JSON column. Flat
columns extracted from it — primary title, seniority, primary employer domain — because
schedules and filters need flat values to reason about. And the operational state that makes
the system self-maintaining: when enrichment was last *attempted*, when it last *succeeded*,
how often it has failed.

| user_id | email | primary_seniority | last_attempted_at | error_count |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| u_481 | jane@gmail.com | c_suite | 2026-07-18 | 0 |
| u_482 | tom@beta.co | ic | 2026-07-18 | 0 |
| u_483 | bounce@old.io | — | 2026-07-11 | 3 |

Row three matters as much as row one: a signup we *couldn't* enrich stays visible, with its
failure count, instead of vanishing into a retry queue somewhere.

## Table 2: `enriched_user_experiences` — the explosion

The profile's employment history — current **and** past — explodes into its own table, one
row per experience, each carrying the `user_id` it came from. Jane becomes five rows, not
one:

| user_id | company | title | is_current | in_crm |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| u_481 | Acme | CTO | true | true |
| u_481 | Beta Corp | Advisor | true | true |
| u_481 | Gamma | Board Member | true | false |
| u_481 | Delta Inc | VP Engineering | false | true |
| u_481 | Epsilon | Senior Engineer | false | false |

This is where the schema stops lying. Nothing was collapsed, nothing was guessed. And because
*past* experiences are rows too, questions that are impossible in a one-row world become
`WHERE` clauses: *"which signups used to work at a current customer?"* is `is_current = false
AND in_crm = true`. Row four — Jane's old VP role at Delta, a customer — is a warm intro
sitting in plain sight.

Each row then enriches independently: match the employer's domain against the CRM, pull the
account owner, whatever your routing needs. Enrichment that would have been fought over by
one row now happens per employer.

## Table 3: `user_profiles` — one lead per (person × current employer)

The distilled table: only successfully enriched users, only current experiences, only rows
that pass your bar (for us: the employer exists in the CRM). Jane appears **twice** — and
that's the feature, not a bug to dedupe away:

| user_id | name | company | title | is_primary_employer | profile_matches_signup_email | signup_email_type |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| u_481 | Jane | Acme | CTO | true | false | personal_email |
| u_481 | Jane | Beta Corp | Advisor | false | false | personal_email |
| u_482 | Tom | Beta Corp | Data Engineer | true | true | work_email |

Two employers, two leads, zero information lost. The three flags on the right are what make
the table consumable without re-deriving anything:

- **`is_primary_employer`** — which of a person's rows is their main gig. Route and score
  that one; treat the others as relationship context.
- **`profile_matches_signup_email`** — does the signup email's domain equal *this row's*
  employer domain. Tom signed up with `tom@beta.co` and this row is Beta Corp: he signed up
  *as* Beta Corp. That's the highest-intent row in the table — the person used this
  employer's identity to enter your product.
- **`signup_email_type`** — `work_email` or `personal_email`. Jane's gmail signup tells you
  nothing about *which* hat she was wearing; Tom's work email tells you exactly. Downstream
  scoring should trust the two kinds differently.

All three are cheap to compute and impossible to reconstruct later if you didn't keep the
signup email's facts flowing through the pipeline. Which brings us to the workflow.

## The workflow

On the driver table, per row, in order:

1. **Classify the signup email** (work vs personal) and **extract its domain**. Two
   deterministic steps against the one fact the user gave you.
2. **Reverse email lookup** — email in, full standardized profile out, in one call.
3. **Flatten the signals** — primary title, seniority, employer domain, and the experiences
   array pulled out of the profile JSON into flat columns.
4. **Stamp and count** — write the attempt timestamp; on failure, increment a durable error
   counter; on success, record the success time. (Durable meaning: stored *outside* the row,
   so a re-run can't wipe its own bookkeeping.)
5. **Dedupe on discovered identity** — claim the profile URL. Two signups with different
   emails that resolve to the same human stop here; one human never becomes two sets of
   downstream rows.
6. **Delete downstream, then explode** — remove this user's old rows in tables 2 and 3, then
   fan the experiences array out into table 2, carrying `user_id`, `name`, and the
   signup-email facts onto every exploded row.

On the experiences table: flatten each experience (including the provider's
primary-employer flag), compare employer domain against the signup email's domain, match the
employer against the CRM — then append the qualifying current rows into `user_profiles` with
all three flags riding along.

The ordering in step 6 is load-bearing: deletes sit *after* enrichment in the chain, so a
failed or empty refresh never reaches them. **Replacement happens only on success; failure
degrades to "slightly stale," never to "gone."** That single rule is the difference between
a system you trust and one you babysit.

## Keeping it alive

Enrichment rots. The engine re-checks its own answers on a cadence that reads the data it
maintains:

| Row state | Re-enrich after |
|---|---|
| Manager and above | 3 months |
| Individual contributor | 9 months |
| Failed (fewer than 3 failures) | 7 days |
| No result found | 3 months |

Seniority drives urgency — senior people's moves matter more and happen more consequentially
— which is exactly why seniority had to be a flat column on the driver table: the scheduler
filters on it. Failures retry quickly but are capped by the durable counter; a permanently
dead row stops costing money after three strikes. And every successful refresh flows through
the same delete-then-explode replacement, so tables 2 and 3 are always a projection of the
latest good data.

## The payoff

One schema decision — experiences as rows, profiles as (person × employer) — and everything
downstream gets simpler: no lossy "primary title" guessing, no lead information destroyed at
write time, warm-intro queries as plain filters, and per-row flags that tell every consumer
exactly how to read what they're looking at.

We've folded this architecture into [pipe0](https://pipe0.com) as a playbook — the agent
knows it as *Application user enrichment* and assembles all three tables, the workflow, and
the freshness schedules from one prompt:

> *"We push every new product signup into a sheet with user_id, name, and email. Set up user
> enrichment that keeps itself fresh: LinkedIn profile from the email, one row per work
> experience in its own sheet, match each employer against our CRM, and a user_profiles
> sheet of current roles — flagging each profile with the primary employer and whether the
> signup email belongs to it."*

But the architecture is tool-agnostic. However you build it: three tables, explode the
experiences, never collapse a person into one lead — and never let a failed refresh delete
what a successful one wrote.
